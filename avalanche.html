The Debt Avalanche Method

What is the Debt Avalanche Method?

The debt avalanche method is a debt repayment strategy that focuses on paying off debts with the highest interest rates first. This method is mathematically optimal, saving you the most money in interest charges over time.

How Does It Work?

  1. List all your debts from highest to lowest interest rate
  2. Make minimum payments on all debts
  3. Put any extra money toward the highest-interest debt
  4. Once the highest-interest debt is paid off, add that payment to the next highest-interest debt's minimum payment
  5. Repeat until all debts are paid

Example of the Avalanche Method

Let's say you have the following debts:

  • Credit Card A: $4,000 at 22.9% APR (minimum payment $100)
  • Credit Card B: $500 at 19.9% APR (minimum payment $25)
  • Personal Loan: $2,500 at 12.5% APR (minimum payment $75)
  • Car Loan: $10,000 at 5.9% APR (minimum payment $200)

Step-by-Step Process:

  1. Pay minimums on all debts ($400 total)
  2. Add extra money to Credit Card A payment (highest interest at 22.9%)
  3. Once Credit Card A is paid, add its $100 to Credit Card B payment (next highest at 19.9%)
  4. Continue the pattern until all debts are paid

Advantages of the Avalanche Method

  • Saves the most money in interest charges
  • Mathematically the most efficient approach
  • Reduces total debt faster
  • More predictable payoff timeline
  • Works well for those motivated by mathematical optimization

Potential Drawbacks

  • May take longer to see first debt paid off
  • Could be less motivating if highest-interest debts are large
  • Requires discipline to stick with the plan

Is the Avalanche Method Right for You?

The avalanche method might be your best choice if:

  • You're motivated by saving the most money possible
  • You have high-interest debt that's growing quickly
  • You can stay committed without quick wins
  • You prefer a mathematically optimal approach

Avalanche vs. Snowball

While the avalanche method saves more money, the snowball method might provide more motivation through quick wins. Consider your personal motivation style when choosing between them. Some people even combine both methods, starting with snowball for momentum and switching to avalanche later.

Making It Work

For success with the avalanche method:

  • Track your progress regularly
  • Calculate interest saved as motivation
  • Consider balance transfers for high-interest debt
  • Stay committed to the plan even when progress feels slow

Remember that while the avalanche method is mathematically superior, the best debt repayment strategy is ultimately the one you'll stick with consistently.
